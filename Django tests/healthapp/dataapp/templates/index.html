    • Diabetes

    Diabetes is a chronic health condition characterized by elevated levels of blood glucose (sugar) in the body. This occurs either when the pancreas doesn't produce enough insulin (Type 1 diabetes) or when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces (Type 2 diabetes). Insulin is a hormone that helps regulate blood sugar levels and allows glucose to enter cells to provide energy. Uncontrolled diabetes can lead to serious complications affecting various organs, including the eyes, kidneys, nerves, and heart. Symptoms of diabetes may include increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision, and slow wound healing.

    Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

    Hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, is a condition where the force of blood against the walls of the arteries is consistently too high. This elevated pressure can strain the heart, blood vessels, and organs over time, increasing the risk of serious health problems such as heart disease, stroke, and kidney damage. Hypertension often develops over years and usually has no symptoms, earning it the nickname "silent killer." Lifestyle factors, genetic predisposition, and certain medical conditions can contribute to high blood pressure. It can be managed with lifestyle changes, medication, and regular monitoring. Both diabetes and hypertension are chronic conditions that require ongoing management, including lifestyle modifications, medication adherence (if prescribed), regular monitoring, and, in some cases, medical intervention to prevent complications and maintain overall health.
